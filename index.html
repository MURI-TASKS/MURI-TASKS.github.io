Tensor Approaches for Simulating Kinetic Systems (TASKS)

Modern challenges faced by the Department of Defense (DoD) demand a deep understanding of high-dimensional complex systems, characterized by non-stationary partial differential equations (PDEs). Examples include the kinetic theory in modeling probability density functions arise from describing particle distributions in nonequilibrium plasma, and from describing distribution functions of stochastic quantities and observables for quantification of uncertainty and optimal control. While deep learning has gained momentum in solving high-dimensional PDEs, direct accurate simulations remain a challenge. Tensor network approximation stands out as a promising method to combat the curse of dimensionality. Our research seeks to offer a fresh perspective on tensor network computations for high-dimensional complex PDEs, leverage upon and complements state-of-the-art developments from scientific machine learning, tensor networks, reduced order modeling, and uncertainty quantification. Our team develops an interlocking research framework systematically to advance tensor network methods for simulating high-dimensional kinetic models. While the research is inspired by nonequilibrium plasmas simulations and control of plasma instabilities, the underlying methodology promises wide-ranging applications, extending to multiple areas critical to the DoD’s interests and addressing key challenges on high-dimensional non-stationary kinetic simulations. Our team members' collective expertise encompasses multi-scale modeling, low-rank tensor methods, tensor network computations, uncertainty quantification, and machine learning. While plasma applications drive our agenda, the shared vision is broader: creating a holistic framework and research ecosystem on low-rank tensor network methods for high-dimensional kinetic models.
